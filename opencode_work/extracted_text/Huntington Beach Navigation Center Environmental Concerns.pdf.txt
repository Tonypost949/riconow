Executive Summary
The intersection of emergency municipal housing initiatives and brownfield redevelopment
frequently introduces complex environmental and regulatory challenges. The Huntington Beach
Navigation Center (HBNC), located at 17642 Beach Boulevard and 17631 Cameron Lane,
represents a critical and escalating case study in the catastrophic failure of institutional
engineering controls and the ensuing public health ramifications. Acquired and developed in
the summer of 2020 to rapidly address the regional homelessness crisis, the facility was
erected over a heavily contaminated industrial and agricultural site. Rather than undertaking a
comprehensive, deep-soil forensic excavation to physically remove the hazardous waste,
municipal authorities and their retained environmental consultants secured a conditional
closure from the Orange County Health Care Agency (OCHCA). This regulatory clearance was
predicated entirely on the installation and continuous maintenance of an asphalt containment
cap designed to entomb the subterranean toxins.
Recent whistleblower actions, formalized in California State Water Resources Control Board
Petition A-2926 and a subsequent federal civil rights and environmental lawsuit (Case No.
8:26-cv-00348), allege that the facility operator has systematically and negligently degraded
this protective asphalt cap through daily high-pressure washing operations. This mechanical
shear has purportedly resulted in the continuous aerosolization of severe underlying
toxins—most notably hexavalent chromium at concentrations documented at 49 times the
allowable residential safety limit, alongside dangerous concentrations of lead, arsenic, and
legacy agricultural pesticides such as Toxaphene and Dichlorodiphenyldichloroethylene (DDE).1
The chronic inhalation and dermal exposure to this toxic plume has reportedly caused severe,
permanent physiological injuries to shelter residents, including pleural effusion, sepsis, and
pneumonitis.1
This exhaustive research report provides a forensic analysis of the site's historical land use, the
2020 environmental due diligence parameters, the precise hydro-mechanical mechanisms of
cap degradation and aerosolization, the toxicological profile of the mobilized contaminants,
and the current administrative and federal legal frameworks governing the ongoing dispute as
of late February 2026.
Part I: Historical Stratigraphy and the Genesis of
Subsurface Contamination
The severe soil contamination profile beneath the Gothard Street and Beach Boulevard
corridor in Huntington Beach is not the result of a singular localized dumping event or an
isolated industrial accident. Rather, it is the cumulative, stratigraphic byproduct of over a
century of overlapping, intensive land uses. The subsurface pollution matrix mirrors the
economic and demographic evolution of Orange County, transitioning sequentially from heavy
agriculture to fossil fuel extraction, and ultimately to industrial manufacturing and mineral
mining. Understanding this historical progression is vital for comprehending the specific
chemical cocktail currently threatening the Huntington Beach Navigation Center.
The Agricultural Legacy of the Wintersburg Settlement
In the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, the area encompassing the current
Navigation Center was an integral part of the agricultural settlement of Wintersburg, a
community centered near the intersection of present-day Gothard Street and Warner Avenue.2
Formally subdivided in 1906 by Orange County Surveyor S.H. Finley, Wintersburg evolved into a
thriving agrarian enclave, driven largely by pioneering Japanese American farmers who
expertly cultivated the region's unique, nutrient-dense peatlands.3 The soil in this specific
corridor, once considered un-farmable marshland, was drained and transformed into a vital
agricultural hub renowned for the mass production of celery, sugar beets, and chili peppers.3
However, the intensive farming practices of the early-to-mid twentieth century, designed to
maximize crop yields in the highly competitive Southern California agricultural market, relied
upon the aggressive and continuous application of early-generation agrochemicals. The
historical use of organochlorine and organophosphate pesticides, broad-spectrum herbicides,
fungicides, and inorganic biocides became deeply and permanently embedded in the local soil
matrix.5 The legacy of this agricultural era is most prominently marked by the highly persistent
presence of Toxaphene and DDE—a highly stable breakdown product of the infamous
insecticide DDT.1
Furthermore, the inorganic pesticides heavily relied upon during this era to combat celery
blight and other crop diseases introduced massive quantities of arsenic into the shallow soil
strata.1 Unlike organic compounds that may slowly degrade over decades through microbial
action, arsenic is a fundamental element that remains indefinitely static in the soil profile unless
physically removed. Consequently, the Wintersburg agricultural boom created a baseline of
severe toxicological hazard—a foundational layer of pesticide and heavy metal
contamination—long before the region's industrialization even commenced.
Industrialization: The 1920s Oil Boom and Petrochemical Infiltration
The demographic and economic profile of Huntington Beach shifted violently following the
discovery of substantial crude oil reserves. On May 24, 1920, the Huntington A-1 well was
brought into production, marking the inception of the Huntington Beach Oil Field.7 This field
quickly became one of the most prolific in the Los Angeles basin, triggering a massive, frenzied
economic boom that transformed the quiet coastal and agricultural communities into a heavily
industrialized zone characterized by densely packed oil derricks.7
The Gothard Street corridor and the surrounding wetlands were rapidly enveloped by this
unregulated extractive industry. The early decades of oil exploration were characterized by
rudimentary drilling technology, an absence of environmental containment protocols, and a
general disregard for ecological preservation. The extraction, unrefined storage, and transport
of crude oil introduced a vast array of highly mobile petroleum hydrocarbons and volatile
organic compounds (VOCs) into the environment.5 Historical accounts document toxic sludge
filling local creeks and spontaneous combustion events occurring in the heavily polluted peat
bogs.9 The permeation of crude oil byproducts into the soil compounded the existing
agricultural contamination, adding highly reactive chemical solvents to the subterranean matrix
beneath what is now the Navigation Center.
Sand and Gravel Mining and Asphalt Manufacturing
Simultaneously, the geological composition of the Gothard Street corridor made it an ideal
location for extensive sand and gravel mining. Large-scale open-pit quarrying operations,
including the Sully Miller lake and the nearby Bruce Brothers sand pit, excavated massive
swaths of earth from the local terrain.10 These deep excavations fundamentally altered the local
hydrology and created vast, depressed areas that were subsequently repurposed for heavy
industrial applications.
By the mid-twentieth century, the immediate vicinity of the subject property at Cameron Lane
and Beach Boulevard housed intensive industrial facilities, including hot-mix asphalt plants,
diesel spray rack areas, heavy equipment maintenance shops, a laboratory for testing raw
materials, and numerous underground storage tanks (USTs) and aboveground storage tanks
(ASTs).5 The Sully Miller Contracting Company, and its affiliate Blue Diamond Materials,
operated a sprawling asphalt production plant on the site, utilizing highly caustic chemical
solvents, industrial agents, and heavy machinery until the facility was finally decommissioned
and demolished in 1995.5
This combination of unrefined fossil fuel processing, aggregate mining, and asphalt
manufacturing introduced the most dangerous contaminants into the ground. The most
hazardous byproducts of these specific industrial operations were severe heavy
metals—specifically lead and hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)).1 The historical regulatory record
demonstrates that the site was subjected to several environmental investigations between
April 1986 and January 1987 due to documented fuel spills and leaking USTs at the Sully Miller
facility.5 While fuel hydrocarbon-affected soil was excavated in localized areas where the
immediate spills occurred, these limited remediation efforts failed to address the massive,
site-wide dispersion of aerosolized heavy metals and legacy agricultural poisons that had
accumulated over the previous century.5

Part II: The 2020 Municipal Acquisition and Expedited
Development
As the State of California faced a rapidly escalating, highly visible homelessness crisis in the late
2010s, municipal governments faced immense political and legal pressure to rapidly expand
temporary shelter capacity. To satisfy directives from the County of Orange requiring every
municipality to fair-share the burden of regional homelessness, the City of Huntington Beach
committed to establishing a Homeless Navigation Center.13 In early 2020, the City targeted the
adjacent, vacant properties at 17631 Cameron Lane and 17642 Beach Boulevard for the
expedited construction of this vital facility.6
EEC Environmental Due Diligence and the Discovery of the Plume
To facilitate the rapid acquisition and development of the site, the City of Huntington Beach
entered into a professional services agreement with EEC Environmental, a specialized
environmental engineering and consulting firm. Because the City was operating under a
self-imposed mandate to open the facility by November 2020, the firm was retained on an
emergency procurement basis to perform the requisite environmental due diligence and
construction oversight.13 The initial contract, awarded in August 2020, was valued at a
not-to-exceed (NTE) amount of $63,000; however, as the complexity of the site's
contamination became apparent, the contract was subsequently amended in December 2020
with an additional $75,000, bringing the total compensation to $138,000.13
Extensive soil sampling, bore testing, and laboratory analysis conducted during the summer of
2020 confirmed the presence of severe historical contamination. The testing identified a
massive, highly concentrated toxic plume beneath the surface containing elevated levels of
agricultural pesticides (Toxaphene and DDE), arsenic, lead, and hexavalent chromium.6 The
environmental findings presented the municipality with a profound logistical and financial
dilemma. Given the severity and depth of the heavy metal concentrations, standard
environmental remediation protocols under California law would typically mandate a
comprehensive deep-soil excavation and the physical removal of the hazardous waste to an
off-site Class I hazardous waste disposal facility.
However, a full-scale forensic excavation presented two insurmountable obstacles for the city's
political and operational objectives: it would require exorbitant financial outlays well beyond the
allocated budget for the shelter, and it would severely delay the project, pushing the opening
well past the mandated November/December 2020 deadline.13
The Regulatory Compromise: OCHCA's Conditional "Case Closed"
Determination
To circumvent the massive delays and prohibitive costs associated with deep-soil remediation,
the City of Huntington Beach and EEC Environmental engaged in extensive negotiations with
the Orange County Health Care Agency (OCHCA), which acted as the local regulatory
oversight body for the site.6 Following the submission of the environmental findings and
additional random soil sampling conducted in May 2020, the OCHCA issued a conditional
"Case Closed" determination.6
In environmental regulatory practice, a conditional closure does not imply that a site has been
cleaned or that the hazardous waste has been neutralized. Rather, it is a risk-management
mechanism that allows a contaminated site to be utilized for specific, restricted purposes,
provided that strict institutional and engineering controls are implemented and continuously
maintained to prevent human exposure to the underlying hazards.
In the case of the Huntington Beach Navigation Center, the OCHCA deemed the highly toxic
site safe for the operation of an emergency human shelter facility under one strict,
non-negotiable condition: the installation and continuous maintenance of an impermeable
asphalt pavement cover.6 This asphalt cap was engineered to serve as a physical isolation
barrier, effectively entombing the underlying pesticides and heavy metals in perpetuity. The
theoretical logic of this engineering control was that, as long as the asphalt remained entirely
intact, it would prevent the shelter's future inhabitants from direct dermal contact with the soil,
ingestion of contaminated dirt, or inhalation of hazardous dust.6
Relying entirely on this fragile conditional closure, the City proceeded with the rapid
development of the site. To optimize the footprint and accelerate construction, the City
collaborated with Sprung, utilizing prefabricated, fully insulated tensioned membrane
structures. Combining a 60' x 105' structure with a 60' x 90' structure, they erected a
continuous 60' x 195' facility capable of housing 174 adults.15 Crucially, these Sprung structures
do not utilize deep, traditional concrete foundations, placing an even greater reliance on the
integrity of the surface-level asphalt cap to protect the interior environment from subsurface
vapor intrusion and particulate exposure. Operations of the completed facility were contracted
to Mercy House Living Centers, and the HBNC officially opened its doors in December 2020.14
Part III: Operational Failure and the Mechanics of Cap
Degradation
The efficacy of an asphalt containment cap in a highly contaminated brownfield redevelopment
is entirely and unyieldingly contingent upon its absolute structural integrity. Unlike heavily
reinforced concrete, asphalt is a visco-elastic material; it is inherently semi-porous and highly
susceptible to mechanical shear, chemical degradation, and thermal weathering over time. The
core of the current environmental crisis and ensuing litigation at the Huntington Beach
Navigation Center stems directly from the alleged operational mismanagement of this highly
sensitive, life-critical containment barrier.
Facility Operations and Sanitation Pressures
Mercy House Living Centers assumed full operational control of the HBNC, providing intensive
case management, sheltering, dining, and hygiene services to up to 174 unhoused individuals at
any given time.15 The daily operations of a high-density, low-barrier emergency shelter
inevitably require rigorous sanitation and cleaning protocols to manage biological waste,
accumulated debris, and general facility upkeep. However, whistleblower documentation
indicates a fatal disconnect between the shelter's aggressive operational sanitation practices
and the strict, legally mandated environmental limitations dictated by the OCHCA conditional
closure.
Treating the asphalt cap as if it were a standard, non-sensitive commercial parking lot, the
facility management allegedly implemented cleaning protocols that systematically destroyed
the barrier they were legally obligated to protect.
The Physics of Cap Failure: High-Pressure Washing and Hydroblasting
According to formally filed legal petitions with state regulatory boards, the facility operator,
Mercy House, has routinely and repeatedly utilized high-pressure washing (often referred to
industrially as hydroblasting) to clean the exterior grounds and asphalt surfaces of the
Navigation Center.1 High-pressure cleaning systems generate highly focused water streams at
extreme velocities—frequently ranging from 1,000 to over 3,000 pounds per square inch (psi),
with some industrial units exceeding 10,000 psi.18
From an environmental engineering and materials science perspective, the application of
high-pressure water streams to a thin, non-structural asphalt cap overlying a shallow
hazardous waste plume is a catastrophic operational failure. The physical mechanics of this
barrier degradation occur in three distinct, sequential phases:
1. Binder Emulsification and Aggregate Stripping: The immense kinetic energy of the
high-pressure water stream physically attacks the structural integrity of the asphalt.
Asphalt pavement consists of rock aggregate held together by a highly viscous bitumen
binder. The water jet slices into the micro-fissures of the asphalt, physically stripping the
bitumen binder from the aggregate matrix. This rapidly accelerates the weathering
process, causing the asphalt to become highly brittle, highly porous, to spider-crack, and
ultimately to disintegrate into loose gravel.
2. Hydraulic Scouring of the Sub-base: Once the superficial asphalt barrier is breached
and its impermeability compromised, the high-pressure water penetrates directly into the
sub-base, making violent physical contact with the highly contaminated historical soil. The
immense hydraulic force churns the underlying earth, mixing the entrenched heavy metals
(lead, chromium) and hydrophobic pesticides (Toxaphene, DDE) into a highly turbulent
liquid slurry.
3. Aerosolization and Particulate Suspension: As the high-pressure spray impacts the
ground and reflects upward, it causes massive kinetic resuspension. The mechanical shear
force shatters the contaminated water slurry into microscopic droplets, carrying sub-180
micrometer soil particulates violently into the air.19
This final process, known scientifically as aerosolization, effectively transforms static, safely
encapsulated subterranean soil contaminants into an actively inhalable, highly mobile toxic mist.
By systematically breaking down the cap through daily or weekly hydroblasting, the pressure
washing operations have allegedly mobilized the underlying heavy metals, continuously
pushing them into the ambient air where they drift into the shelter's ventilation intakes, as well
as washing them directly into the municipal stormwater runoff system.1
Part IV: Toxicological Profiling and Public Health
Endangerment
The aerosolization of the specific chemical constituents found beneath the Huntington Beach
Navigation Center poses an imminent, severe, and legally actionable endangerment to human
health. When heavy metals and highly stable organochlorine pesticides are suspended in the
ambient air as fine particulate matter (specifically PM10 and PM2.5), they bypass the human
body's primary dermal and digestive defense mechanisms. Upon inhalation, these microscopic
toxic particulates enter directly into the deep pulmonary system, cross the alveolar-capillary
barrier, and absorb directly into the bloodstream, initiating rapid systemic poisoning.
Hexavalent Chromium (Cr(VI)): Extreme Toxicity and Carcinogenicity
The most critical and alarming hazard identified in the soil at the HBNC site is Hexavalent
Chromium (Cr(VI)). According to the environmental filings in Petition A-2926, Cr(VI) was
documented in the underlying soil at levels an staggering 49 times the allowable safety limit.1
To understand the severity of this exposure, one must differentiate between the atomic states
of chromium. Trivalent chromium (Cr(III)) is a naturally occurring trace element and an essential
human dietary nutrient. Conversely, hexavalent chromium (Cr(VI)) is a highly toxic, entirely
anthropogenic byproduct of severe industrial processes, most notably electroplating, metal
finishing, and the specific types of hot-mix asphalt production historically conducted at the
Sully Miller site.22
The California Department of Toxic Substances Control (DTSC), the Office of Environmental
Health Hazard Assessment (OEHHA), and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA)
universally classify Cr(VI) as a known, exceptionally potent human carcinogen, particularly
when the exposure pathway is inhalation.22 The DTSC Human and Ecological Risk Office (HERO)
sets remarkably low, highly restrictive residential soil screening levels (often measured in
fractions of a milligram per kilogram) for Cr(VI) precisely due to its extreme genotoxicity and
mutagenicity.26
The National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health (NIOSH) dictates that even
short-term, low-dose inhalation of Cr(VI) aerosols leads to severe respiratory degradation,
occupational asthma, rhinitis, and perforated nasal septa. Chronic exposure dramatically
increases the incidence rates of lung and sinus cancer.23 Furthermore, Cr(VI) is a heavily
documented reproductive toxicant, capable of penetrating cell membranes and causing
profound intracellular oxidative stress and DNA damage.24 The verified presence of this
compound at 49 times the allowable residential threshold, combined with forced, mechanical
aerosolization via high-pressure washing, creates a continuous, high-level inhalation exposure
event for anyone residing, working, or breathing within the aerosol drift zone of the Navigation
Center.1
The Synergistic Pathology of Lead, Arsenic, and Pesticides
While Cr(VI) presents the most acute and horrifying carcinogenic risk, the site's aerosolized soil
plume also contains highly dangerous concentrations of lead, arsenic, and the legacy
pesticides Toxaphene and DDE.1 The simultaneous inhalation of these compounds creates a
devastating synergistic effect on the human body.
● Lead: A pervasive legacy of the site's industrial past, lead exposure, particularly through
the inhalation of fine dust, causes profound and irreversible neurological damage, severe
cardiovascular stress, and renal (kidney) failure. DTSC soil screening protocols heavily
restrict lead levels due to its insidious bioaccumulative nature, meaning the human body
cannot efficiently excrete it, leading to lifelong toxicity.27
● Arsenic: A highly toxic inorganic metalloid linked directly to the site's Wintersburg-era
pesticide use. The inhalation of arsenic dust severely irritates the pulmonary tract,
contributes directly to cardiovascular disease, and is a known systemic organ toxicant and
carcinogen.1
● Toxaphene and DDE: These highly persistent organochlorine agricultural pesticides act as
powerful endocrine disruptors and neurotoxins. They resist environmental degradation,
meaning the chemicals applied in 1930 are entirely as lethal when inhaled in 2026.1
Documented Physiological Trauma and Whistleblower Illness
The catastrophic human cost of this synergistic toxic exposure is not merely theoretical; it is
actively documented in the legal filings surrounding the facility. According to Petition A-2926,
the continuous aerosolization of these heavy metals has resulted in severe, disabling, and
permanent personal injuries to residents.1
The whistleblower, Jesse Knabb, documented a horrifying array of medical pathologies
acquired during residency at the site. These include pleural effusion (a dangerous build-up of
fluid between the layers of tissue that line the lungs and chest cavity), pneumonitis (severe
inflammation of the actual lung tissue), renal impairment (kidney damage highly consistent with
lead and chromium processing), sepsis, leukocytosis (a significantly elevated white blood cell
count indicating massive, systemic immune response to infection or toxic inflammation),
extreme anemia, pneumonia, and empyema (the collection of pus in the pleural cavity requiring
highly invasive surgical intervention known as thoracentesis).1
From a clinical and toxicological standpoint, this specific constellation of severe respiratory,
renal, and systemic immune pathologies is directly, unambiguously consistent with the acute
and chronic inhalation of caustic, metal-laden, pesticide-rich aerosols.

Part V: Administrative Whistleblower Interventions
and Regulatory Paralysis
In direct response to the visibly deteriorating environmental conditions at the site and the
severe, life-altering health impacts experienced by the residents, significant legal and
administrative actions were initiated to force regulatory intervention. The primary mechanism
of this intervention began with the filing of a formal, highly detailed petition with the California
State Water Resources Control Board.
Petition A-2926: Core Allegations and Documented Endangerment
On November 17, 2025, an aggrieved party and former resident, Jesse Knabb, formally filed
Petition A-2926 against the California Regional Water Quality Control Board, Santa Ana Region
(Region 8).1 Knabb, acting as a whistleblower to expose the ongoing environmental
catastrophe, submitted the petition to demand immediate action regarding the continuous
Imminent and Substantial Endangerment (ISE) posed by the degraded site.1
The petition meticulously outlined severe, ongoing violations of the federal Clean Water Act (§
1311). Specifically, it alleged that the daily destruction of the asphalt containment cap via Mercy
House's high-pressure washing operations constituted the continuous, unpermitted, and illegal
disposal and mobilization of hazardous waste.1 Furthermore, the petition exposed a profound
bureaucratic failure: the HBNC facility was allegedly operating under an expired Stormwater
Permit (NPDES Order No. R8-2009-0030, which expired over five years prior on August 20,
2020).1 This meant that the highly contaminated, metal-laden runoff generated by the pressure
washing operations was entering the municipal stormwater system entirely illegally and without
regulatory monitoring.
The petitioner demanded immediate, mandatory enforcement actions from the State,
including:
1. The immediate issuance of a formal Cleanup and Abatement Order against the City of
Huntington Beach and Mercy House Living Centers to instantly cease all high-pressure
washing activities.1
2. The immediate abatement of the illegal disposal of hazardous waste into the air and
runoff.1
3. The imposition of maximum statutory civil penalties for the continuous, documented
operational violations of the Clean Water Act.1
4. The mandate of a full, independent forensic deep-soil excavation to physically remove the
plume and remediate the site to proper, objective safety standards.
Regulatory Paralysis and Dismissal by Operation of Law
The administrative process surrounding Petition A-2926 highlights a profound and disturbing
level of regulatory paralysis within California's environmental oversight agencies. The petition
was filed as a direct legal challenge to the Santa Ana Regional Water Quality Control Board's
explicit refusal to act on prior, repeated notices regarding the continuous operation of an
unpermitted toxic discharge on a known, documented hazardous waste site.1
Upon formal receipt of the petition on November 17, 2025, the State Water Board placed it
under consideration for review. Under California administrative law, this triggered a strict,
non-negotiable statutory deadline of February 16, 2026.29 According to the California Code of
Regulations (title 23, section 2050.5, subdivision (e)), active water quality petitions are
governed by a definitive, highly rigid operational statute: if the State Water Board does not
explicitly issue a formal letter granting review of the petition, and the petitioner does not
formally submit a written request for the petition to be held in abeyance prior to 5:00 p.m. on
the exact deadline day, the petition is automatically and permanently "dismissed by operation
of law".29
This specific bureaucratic mechanism—dismissal by operation of law—allows regulatory
agencies to effectively ignore, sideline, or silence highly contentious or politically damaging
environmental petitions without ever having to issue a formal, written denial or substantively
engage with the horrifying medical evidence of public endangerment. As the February 16,
2026, deadline approached and ultimately passed without the State Water Board taking the
affirmative action required to grant a review, the administrative avenues for remediation at the
state level effectively closed, completely abandoning the endangered residents and forcing the
petitioner to seek immediate judicial relief.
Part VI: Escalation to the Federal Judiciary and
Municipal Ramifications
Recognizing the impending, inevitable dismissal of the administrative petition due to State
Water Board inaction, and driven by the urgent, life-or-death necessity to halt the daily
aerosolization of hexavalent chromium and lead, the legal strategy pivoted sharply to the
federal judiciary.
The Federal RCRA Complaint: Knabb v. City of Huntington Beach et al.
On Saturday, February 14, 2026—calculatedly filed just two days prior to the State Water
Board's final dismissal deadline—plaintiff Jesse Knabb filed a massive, comprehensive 59-page
federal civil complaint in the United States District Court for the Central District of California.31
The federal lawsuit, officially docketed as Case No. 8:26-cv-00348, was assigned to District
Judge John W. Holcomb, with all discovery matters referred to Magistrate Judge Autumn D.
Spaeth.31 The nature of the suit is formally classified under "893 Other Statutes - Environmental
Matters," with the specific, aggressive cause of action filed under 42 U.S.C. § 6901.31 This statute
represents the core provisions of the Resource Conservation and Recovery Act (RCRA)
governing Environmental Cleanup Expenses, citizen suits, and the legal mechanisms to halt
imminent and substantial hazards to public health and the environment.
Concurrently, Knabb filed a formal request to Proceed In Forma Pauperis (CV-60) alongside a
Declaration in Support.31 This filing officially indicates the plaintiff's indigent status—a direct,
tragic consequence of the severe, permanent physical disabilities and medical bankruptcy
allegedly caused by the toxic exposure suffered while residing at the city-operated shelter.1 The
plaintiff has also formally demanded a trial by jury.31
The Defendant Matrix and Alleged Liabilities
The federal litigation strategy is exceptionally broad, systematically targeting every single
municipal, corporate, and regulatory entity responsible for the chain of custody, the failed
oversight, and the negligent operation of the contaminated site. The liability profile for each
defendant is distinct and profound:
1. The City of Huntington Beach: As the primary property owner and the municipality that
commissioned and funded the site, the city faces massive primary liability for placing a
highly vulnerable, captive population on an inadequately remediated brownfield, and
subsequently failing to monitor or enforce the integrity of the critical institutional
controls.31
2. Mercy House Living Centers, Inc.: As the contracted site operator, Mercy House is
directly implicated as the primary active tortfeasor responsible for the physical
destruction of the asphalt cap via high-pressure washing and the subsequent, daily
aerosolization of the highly toxic plume.1
3. EEC Environmental: The consulting firm that conducted the initial 2020 due diligence and
recommended the fragile asphalt cap strategy instead of deep excavation.13 Their liability
will likely hinge on professional negligence and whether the recommended engineering
control was ever objectively sufficient or scientifically sound for the known, high-traffic
end-use of the site.
4. Orange County Health Care Agency (OCHCA): The local regulatory agency that issued
the fatal conditional "Case Closed" finding, allowing the shelter to be built over the toxins.6
5. California Regional Water Quality Control Board, Santa Ana Region 8: Targeted heavily
for their profound bureaucratic failure to enforce the Clean Water Act, ignoring
whistleblower complaints, and allowing unpermitted, highly toxic stormwater discharges
to persist for years.1
6. Mitsuru Yamada: Named as an individual defendant, potentially related to historic land
ownership, previous lease agreements, or operational oversight prior to the city's
acquisition.31

Broader Municipal Context and Political Irony
The federal court's involvement marks a massive escalation of the crisis, shifting the venue
from obscure, easily dismissed administrative bureaucratic procedures to the highest levels of
federal environmental law, which possesses vastly more robust mechanisms for aggressive
discovery, immediate injunctive relief, and severe punitive damages.
This environmental disaster must also be viewed through the lens of Huntington Beach's
broader, highly controversial municipal politics regarding homelessness. The current City
Council majority was elected on a pledge to implement an aggressive 90-day plan to "clean up
the streets" and exert maximum control over the unhoused population.32 Their strategy relies
heavily on the strict enforcement of anti-camping ordinances, effectively criminalizing sleeping
in public spaces and utilizing police enforcement to forcefully push homeless individuals into
the Huntington Beach Navigation Center.32 Other municipalities, such as Escondido, have even
toured the facility, viewing HB's strategy of strict enforcement and sheltering as a model to
emulate.34
However, the federal lawsuit exposes a dark, tragic irony in this municipal strategy: by enforcing
anti-camping laws to push people off the streets, the City of Huntington Beach is using the
force of law to funnel its most vulnerable citizens directly into an active, highly lethal toxic
exposure zone. In late February 2026, the Mayor of Huntington Beach issued statements
regarding housing lawsuits 35, indicating that the city is currently besieged by legal challenges
regarding its housing and homelessness policies. The explosion of the HBNC environmental
crisis into federal court will exponentially compound these political and legal vulnerabilities.
Part VII: Future Outlook and Remediation Imperatives
The rapidly unfolding crisis at the Huntington Beach Navigation Center represents one of the
most critical failures of environmental justice and municipal planning in recent Southern
California history. By fast-tracking an emergency human shelter on a highly toxic, multi-layered
industrial site using a vastly substandard, cost-saving containment strategy, the city and its
regulatory agencies inadvertently subjected a captive, unhoused population to life-threatening
chemical exposure.
The scientific and hydro-mechanical realities of the site are undeniable: a thin, semi-porous
asphalt cap simply cannot withstand the massive mechanical shear forces of daily industrial
high-pressure washing. As long as the sub-base remains physically breached, the 49x limits of
hexavalent chromium, alongside massive deposits of lead, arsenic, and organochlorine
pesticides, will continue to freely aerosolize. This creates a perpetual, highly lethal threat not
only to the shelter residents and Mercy House staff, but to the surrounding Huntington Beach
community situated within the prevailing wind drift zone.
Moving forward, the federal litigation initiated under RCRA will almost certainly demand the
immediate, court-ordered cessation of all hydroblasting and high-pressure cleaning activities
at the facility. More critically, as discovery proceeds and the full extent of the toxicological
trauma is revealed, the federal court possesses the authority to mandate the exact
environmental action the city desperately sought to avoid in 2020: a comprehensive,
independent, deep-soil forensic excavation of the entire 17642 Beach Boulevard and 17631
Cameron Lane footprint.
Conclusion
The severe environmental deterioration and subsequent human tragedy at the Huntington
Beach Navigation Center fundamentally underscores the catastrophic, unacceptable risks of
utilizing expedited, conditional remediation strategies—such as shallow asphalt capping—for
permanent or semi-permanent human habitation sites. The deep, inescapable legacy of highly
persistent agricultural pesticides from the Wintersburg farming era, fatally compounded by the
massive heavy metal and VOC deposits from twentieth-century oil extraction, gravel mining,
and hot-mix asphalt manufacturing, created a highly volatile, exceptionally dangerous
subterranean environment.
The financially motivated decision to cap the site rather than physically excavate the hazard,
followed immediately by the negligent, daily destruction of that exact cap via high-pressure
washing, catalyzed the continuous aerosolization of hexavalent chromium and lead. This
unbroken chain of municipal, corporate, and regulatory failures directly resulted in severe,
permanent physiological trauma to the shelter's residents.
With all administrative remedies completely exhausted following the California State Water
Board's bureaucratic dismissal of Petition A-2926 by operation of law, the resolution of this
disaster now rests entirely within the federal judiciary. Case No. 8:26-cv-00348 will not only
determine the massive financial and legal liability of the City of Huntington Beach, Mercy
House, EEC Environmental, and regional regulatory bodies, but it will also set a monumental,
nationwide legal precedent regarding municipal accountability, the rigorous limits of brownfield
redevelopment, and the absolute, undeniable environmental rights of unhoused populations.
Until the underlying toxic plume is physically excavated and permanently disposed of in a
licensed Class I hazardous waste facility, the Huntington Beach Navigation Center will remain
an active, lethal public health hazard, fundamentally and tragically undermining its core mission
to provide a safe haven for the community's most vulnerable citizens.
